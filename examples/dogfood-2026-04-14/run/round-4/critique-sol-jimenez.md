# Critique — Sol Jiménez

**Persona:** Tenant organizer, 8 years in, reads theory at night, sick of writers who've never lost a campaign.
**Overall:** This one actually lands — the "Monday version" section is the thing I've been trying to get my co-organizers to hear, and I can forward this draft as ammunition. But the writer flirts with cosplay in a couple of places, and if I'm going to hand it to Marco I need those cleaned up or he'll pull on the loose threads and the whole thing comes off the wall.

## Assumptions stress-test

- **Assumption (load-bearing #1): The BBQ expert is the problem, not the misinformation.** Grant. This is exactly the dynamic I watch kill rooms. The expert wins and loses the building. The audit flagged this as a risk for readers who'd "side with the expert" — those readers aren't me and aren't anyone I organize with. Fine.
- **Assumption (load-bearing #2): Material reality changes minds more reliably than argument.** Grant, and the writer did more work here than the audit gives them credit for — the retrospective-voting paragraph near the end, with the Achen/Bartels hedge, is the minimum honest version of the claim. I'll take it. What I want is more *mechanism* at the organizing level, not more political-science citation.
- **Assumption (load-bearing #3): The Kulturkampf analogy illuminates.** Skeptical. It reads as erudition, not as a working model. I didn't need Bismarck to believe the claim, and the piece never maps the analogy tightly enough for the history to pay rent. I'd survive without it; my co-organizers will skim it.

## Findings

## The Monday version is the best paragraph in the essay — lean harder
**severity:** minor
**summary:** The four-bullet "Monday version" is the load-bearing passage for the organizer audience and deserves more weight, not less.

This is the section I'm actually going to screenshot. "The point of the first conversation is the second conversation" — that's a rap I've taught new canvassers and I've never seen it written down that cleanly. "The walk-over is the recruitment" is the same. If anything I want this moved earlier or at least flagged as the payoff so a reader like me knows to hold on through the Bismarck stuff. The one note: the bullets undersell *turf* — you name it in the canvass paragraph above but not in the bullets. For a reader without the steward vocabulary, "pick one condition you share with the people near you" is almost turf but not quite; a half-sentence connecting bullet one to a neighborhood, a shop floor, a parent group would make it operational instead of aspirational.

## "Action" gets named, but not *disambiguated* between modes
**severity:** material
**summary:** The essay names organizing but doesn't distinguish among strike / mutual aid / electoral / one-on-ones, which is the exact argument I need ammo for.

My fight with my co-organizers is not whether organizing matters. It's whether we should be running another canvass or sitting in the church basement doing political education, and underneath that, whether mutual aid "counts" as building power or is charity with better branding. The essay lumps unions, tenant associations, churches, mutual aid networks, and campaigns into one bucket called "organizing" and then gives me one archetype (the canvasser, the steward, the deacon) per paragraph. That's fine as literature. It is not fine as the ammunition the thesis promises. The essay's own logic — *minds won through action act* — implies that different actions produce different minds, and I want the writer to say so. A rent strike produces a different political subject than a casserole does. Both are real organizing. They are not interchangeable, and the essay's refusal to choose among them is the move I need it *not* to make. Fix: one paragraph, somewhere between "Reciprocating Cycles" and the Monday version, that says *these modes differ, here is what each is good for, here is where I think the writer's sympathies lie and why*. Without it, I can't hand this to Marco; he'll say "see, even this guy thinks canvassing and casseroles are the same thing."

## The barbecue frame risks reading as sympathy for the misinformed
**severity:** minor
**summary:** The opening's affective load tilts toward the spreader and could read, to a less charitable organizer, as excusing bad epistemic behavior.

I grant the frame — the expert lost the room, and that's the point. But I've sat through enough post-mortems to know that some of my co-organizers will read "the spreader merely backpedalled" as the writer going soft on misinformation. The essay's actual claim is *colder* than that: the expert's method doesn't work, regardless of who's right. Say it colder. "The expert was right. He also lost the room. Both things are true and the essay is about the second one." The current version lets the reader infer the writer feels sorry for the spreader, which isn't the argument and weakens the argument by association.

## Stub-ish sections undermine the "skin in the game" signal
**severity:** minor
**summary:** "Hearts and Minds" and the solidarity-vs-critical-reasoning section are short enough that a skeptical organizer reads them as gestures rather than lived claims.

The audit called these out as empty; in the draft I'm reading they have bodies, but they're thin. "Hearts and Minds" is a good frame and the COIN hedge is honest, but the section ends before it earns the load-bearing claim ("you cannot argue someone out of a load-bearing belief"). The solidarity section is actually the strongest political-education claim in the piece and it goes by in two paragraphs. A reader like me is looking for a sign the writer has *been* the person who got called centrifugal in a meeting. One concrete story — a strategy meeting where critical reasoning belonged, an action where it didn't, and what it cost when someone got the timing wrong — would buy you everything. Without it, these sections read as theory.

## Bismarck earns his keep only if losing is described in operational terms
**severity:** minor
**summary:** The Kulturkampf history lands as erudition, not as a working model, because "losing" never gets defined in terms an organizer would recognize.

I don't hate the history. I like the sentence "The technocrat does not convert the populist. He constitutes him." That's a keeper. But the historical section spends a lot of words on Bismarck's specific legal instruments (Jesuit Law, Maigesetze, civil marriage dates) and not enough on *what losing looked like on the ground* — mass attendance up, Zentrum vote share up, Catholic associational density up, whatever the actual retrospective numbers were. As written, "losing" is gestural. An organizer reads retention, turnout, density; give me one of those and the analogy stops being decorative. If that's too much work, cut the legal detail and keep the mechanism sentence; the piece will move faster.

## "Material reality" needs one more concrete example from inside organizing
**severity:** minor
**summary:** The essay relies on retrospective-voting for its evidence base when it could use a single organizing anecdote with numbers.

The retrospective-voting hedge is good — it's the honest version of the claim and I appreciate that the writer didn't pretend the literature is cleaner than it is. But an essay whose whole argument is *conditions move minds* would be stronger with one concrete, on-the-ground case: a rent strike that won a freeze and what happened to the building's political participation the next cycle, a union drive that flipped a shop after management cut hours, a mutual aid network that turned into an electoral base. One case, two numbers, a sentence of causality. That's the thing I could hand to a co-organizer who reads the essay and says "sure, in theory." Without it, the terminal claim lives in political science instead of in the craft.

## "The difference shows up on the third Tuesday" is the line — protect it
**severity:** nit
**summary:** Best line in the piece; don't let a later edit bury it.

No action required, just: this is the line that tells me the writer has been the person setting up chairs at 6:45 in an empty room. Keep it, and if anything, let it be the last line of the Monday version section instead of a mid-paragraph beat.

## The aphorism ending works for me, but only because the Monday version earned it
**severity:** nit
**summary:** "Minds won through action act. Minds won through reason reason." lands *because* the operational bullets preceded it; if the bullets were cut, the aphorism would read as vibes.

Just noting the dependency. If an editor ever pressures the writer to trim the bullets for length, the aphorism goes with them or it turns into exactly the theorist-writing-about-practice thing I'd bounce on.
