# Critique — Dr. Paul Henning

**Persona:** 19th-century Central European historian, small-press author, has a Google alert on "Kulturkampf," tired of the term being weaponized by every side this year.
**Overall:** Round 2 has done substantially more of the historical work — the May Laws are named, the Jesuit expulsion is there, the winding-down through the 1880s is there, the Centre Party is named. I will read on. But the argument still leans on one historical claim that is flatly wrong and one rhetorical move that is politically lopsided in a way the author may not notice. I would not write the blog takedown. I would leave a polite comment under my real name.

## Assumptions stress-test

- **Assumption (load-bearing #3): The Kulturkampf/culture-war analogy is actually illuminating.** Round 2 grants me most of what I wanted: the mapping is no longer "vibe rhyme." The author says explicitly that the *mechanism* being claimed is "institutional power pressuring values produces cohesion in the thing pressed on," and names that mechanism. I provisionally grant it, with the reservation below about whose Kulturkampf this is.
- **Assumption (load-bearing #2): Material reality — not reasoned argument — changes minds.** I grant the direction of the claim; I grant economic retrospective voting as a real thing in the literature. I do not grant that it is "the single most reliable predictor anyone has found... stronger than any combination of messaging variables yet measured." That is overreach. Achen/Bartels made this case hard and it is still contested. A historian is going to read "stronger than any combination of messaging variables yet measured" as the kind of sentence one writes when one has not met a political scientist recently.
- **Assumption (load-bearing #1): The expert-berating-the-spreader is the interesting failure, not the misinformation itself.** I am skeptical of the framing but the essay earns its right to focus there by the end of the "Solidarity and critical reasoning" section. Grant, grudgingly.

## Findings

## The Centre Party's founding date is load-bearing and wrong
**severity:** material
**summary:** Piece claims Bismarck's campaign "brought into existence" the Centre Party; the Zentrum was founded December 1870, before the Kulturkampf.

The line is: *"Bismarck was negotiating votes with the same Centre Party his campaign had brought into existence."* This is the rhetorical payoff of the whole section — and it is not what happened. The Deutsche Zentrumspartei was organized in December 1870, out of the older Prussian Zentrumsfraktion, and contested the 1871 Reichstag elections *before* the Kulturkampf proper began. What the Kulturkampf *did* was swell it, harden its discipline, and convert it from a confessional caucus into a mass bloc — the Zentrum went from roughly 18% to ~27% of the Reichstag by 1874 and held that share for decades. That is the real and more interesting story. "Consolidated it," "nationalized it," "made it politically durable" — any of these are true and serve the author's point. "Brought into existence" is false and it is exactly the kind of false that readers who know the period will catch. Fixing this is one sentence and it actually *strengthens* the argument, because "the pressure made the bloc, not the party" is a subtler and truer claim.

## The analogy has a hidden directionality the essay does not own
**severity:** material
**summary:** The modern "Bismarck" is silently coded as the technocratic-progressive coalition (state, platforms, universities, HR, editorial board); if the analogy is real, it must also apply to institutional power exercised by the other side.

This is the part that will decide whether the piece gets read as an argument or as a team jersey. The Kulturkampf mechanism, if you believe it, is symmetrical: *any* coalition that captures institutional power and uses it to discipline a population on values will harden the thing it presses on. The piece only runs the arrow one direction. Its list of institutional pressers is "the state, the platforms, the universities, the HR department, the editorial board" — which, in the 2020s American reader's mind, has a political valence. It never mentions the other extant cases: state legislatures banning curricula, a Supreme Court rolling back a settled right, school boards pulling books, executive orders disciplining universities, a party apparatus excommunicating heretics over vaccines or Ukraine. These are also Kulturkämpfe by this essay's own definition, and they are live in 2026. If the author believes the mechanism, he has to let it cut both ways in the text, or his readers will reasonably conclude he only believes it when *his* preferred coalition is losing. Bismarck's actual warning is not "don't be the progressive." It is "don't be the institution." Say so.

## "Through the *Maigesetze*" compresses the chronology in a way a historian will squint at
**severity:** minor
**summary:** The piece fuses Jesuit expulsion, civil marriage, and the May Laws into one clause; they were three separate instruments over three years.

The Jesuit Law was 1872. The *Maigesetze* proper were 1873 and dealt with clerical education, appointments, and ecclesiastical discipline. Civil marriage in Prussia came in 1874 and was extended across the Reich in 1875. The piece writes: *"He expelled the Jesuits, put civil marriage under state control, and through the Maigesetze — the May Laws of 1873 — placed clerical training and appointments under Prussian supervision."* The sentence parses as if all three were May-Laws products. They were not. This is a minor inaccuracy but it is the kind that a reader who already knows the period uses to decide whether to trust the rest. A semicolon and a re-ordering fix it without bloat.

## "OG of Realpolitik, patron saint of doing whatever works"
**severity:** minor
**summary:** Bismarck was the practitioner; Ludwig von Rochau coined *Realpolitik* in 1853. "Patron saint" is fine; "OG" isn't quite.

I will not die on this hill. The tonal shift from careful analysis to "OG" registers as a gear change mid-paragraph. It also invites the (small) pedantry that Rochau named the thing and Bismarck embodied it — historians are touchy about this because "Realpolitik" in modern usage has drifted a long way from what Rochau meant (he was a *liberal* using it to describe liberal political strategy, originally). "The practitioner most associated with *Realpolitik*" would do the same work without the wince.

## The overreach on retrospective voting
**severity:** minor
**summary:** "The single most reliable predictor... stronger than any combination of messaging variables yet measured" is stronger than the literature will bear.

Retrospective voting is robustly documented. It is not uncontested as *the* strongest predictor across contexts — there is a real debate (Achen/Bartels vs. their critics; the Gelman-Hill-era work; the recent revival of identity-based models). A sentence as confident as this one will get caught by any reader who has read a political-science blog in the last ten years, and the piece does not need the confidence — "one of the most robust predictors ever measured" does the same job without daring the reader to correct it.

## "Normative Statements" is a vestigial line
**severity:** nit
**summary:** Under "Normcore's core norms," the first line is "Normative Statements" with no follow-through.

Either it is a section header mistakenly left in body text, or it is setting up a beat the piece never completes. The section as a whole is coherent now; this orphan line reads as revision debris.

## "May 2025" without an anchor date in a 2026 read
**severity:** nit
**summary:** A reader in the piece's intended shelf-life will wonder which May 2025 and why it matters.

The specificity is good; the lack of a one-line anchor ("last year," or "a year before I wrote this") means the piece will age awkwardly very fast. Historians are the worst audience for undated recency claims.

## What the piece does well (unsolicited, briefly)
**severity:** nit
**summary:** The honest thing to say: the historical section now earns its keep.

"The May Laws were not dramatically repealed; they were quietly wound down" is the sentence I wanted in round 1 and didn't get. The acknowledgment that Bismarck "was too careful a player to lose cleanly" is the kind of nuance that separates a borrowed suit from an earned metaphor. If the Centre-Party-founding error and the analogy's directionality are fixed, this is a piece I could cite in a footnote without embarrassment.
