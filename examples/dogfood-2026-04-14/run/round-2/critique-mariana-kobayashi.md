## Findings

## "I hadn't heard the term 'culture war' until May 2025"
**severity:** material
**summary:** Narrator-credibility line: the author claims unfamiliarity with a term in mainstream political discourse since Buchanan 1992 / Hunter 1991, which reads as implausible from a writer otherwise swinging Bismarck and economic retrospective voting.

This is the line that makes me put the piece down and check who I'm reading. Mariana is a policy staffer who subscribes to Slow Boring and Yglesias. "Culture war" is thirty-year-old vocabulary in her world. A writer claiming to have first encountered it in May 2025 reads one of two ways: (a) performatively naive, which insults her, or (b) genuinely unfamiliar, which torpedoes his authority to tell her how belief-formation works across populations. Either reading is a problem. The fix is trivial — drop the first-encounter framing and just note the spike in usage, or name that the term's usage *shifted* in May 2025 in her communities. The claim that the word was *new to her* is the problem; the observation about a usage spike is fine.

## "Stronger than any combination of messaging variables yet measured"
**severity:** material
**summary:** Overstated empirical claim about economic retrospective voting presented without citation or caveat; a knowledgeable reader will catch it and discount the piece's closing move.

Mariana has read enough of this literature to know it's more contested than the sentence implies. Grossmann, Hersh, and the whole "retrospective voting works when voters can attribute credit/blame cleanly" strand complicate the flat claim. She also knows that "messaging variables" is doing a lot of work — there's a big difference between a Facebook ad split-test and a sustained canvass program, and recent deep-canvassing work (Kalla & Broockman) actually shows persuasion *is* measurable under the right conditions. The piece's whole closing argument rests on this claim being settled. It isn't. A one-sentence hedge ("in the mass-electorate literature, economic retrospective voting is the most robust predictor anyone has found") lands for her; the current phrasing makes her close the tab thinking the author overplayed his hand right at the end.

## "Normcore's core norms / Normative Statements"
**severity:** material
**summary:** Opening section heading and the bold phrase beneath it do no work and never get connected to the essay; reads as editorial residue from an earlier draft.

I get to that heading, then the bolded "Normative Statements," then nothing that connects either phrase to the BBQ anecdote that follows. This is the carryover problem from an earlier draft — the audit flagged it once. It matters because it's the very first thing the reader sees under the title. Mariana gives a piece about ninety seconds to prove it's not a draft-posing-as-a-post. The opening section heading currently spends that budget on two phrases that don't do anything. The BBQ anecdote is strong enough to carry an opener on its own; either delete the section heading or retitle the section around the anecdote's actual subject (the scold's vocabulary).

**summary:** The "geometry of the problem" reframe is good but the Hearts/Minds split gets asserted more than demonstrated.
