# Critique — Sol Jiménez

**Persona:** Tenant organizer, 8 years in the fight, reads theory at night. Looking for ammunition in a long-running argument with her co-organizers that action does the work political education pretends to do.
**Overall:** I read to the end. I folded corners. I'd quote "minds won through action act." But I can't hand this to my co-organizers as the backup I wanted, because the essay accidentally does the thing it diagnoses — it erases the organizer and treats action like weather.

## Assumptions stress-test

- **Expert-berating-spreader is the problem worth examining:** GRANT, with feeling. This is the fight I have every Tuesday. The middle-class volunteer who parachutes into a tenant meeting with a correction and blows up the room — that's the room I've been building for a year. The writer sees this correctly.

- **Material reality, not reasoned argument, changes minds:** GRANT — it's my whole case. But the piece understates something big. Material reality doesn't just happen. Somebody builds the meeting. Somebody makes the 1:1 feel different from a sales pitch. Somebody runs the debrief where the canvasser learns her pitch wasn't landing. The piece says "action → belonging → belief" as if action falls out of the sky. That's not what a retrospective-voting study measures and it's not how a rent strike happens.

- **Kulturkampf analogy is illuminating:** GRANT, reservedly. I read theory, so the Bismarck section doesn't lose me — I actually enjoyed the Maigesetze detail. But I'd want the modern mapping sharper. The state pressing on the Catholic Church isn't the same shape as an HR department pressing on a dissenting employee. The piece lumps "state, platforms, universities, HR department, editorial board" as equivalent sources of institutional pressure. They aren't. Collapsing those real power differences is the kind of move I expect from people who've never had to distinguish between a landlord, a city attorney, and a judge.

## Findings

## Action is named but never textured

**severity:** material
**summary:** "Unions, churches, a canvass" are three nouns, not three worlds — the essay never enters any of them

The thesis is "minds won through action act." I want this thesis to survive. It can't survive on nouns. Unions recruit — how? Through a rep showing up on the floor when someone gets written up. Churches recruit — how? By a deacon bringing a casserole after a funeral. A canvass recruits — how? (The door-knock line is your one piece of texture and it's a good one.) What never appears in this essay: a rent strike. Eviction defense. Mutual aid during a disaster. A tenant association's first meeting where six people realize they all have the same mold problem. These are the grippiest examples of action doing the work, and they're off the page. If the writer has been in any of those rooms, he should show me. If he hasn't, say so — I'll forgive a theorist who knows he's a theorist more than an essayist who wants me to think he's been there.

I can't hand this to Marcus at our next debrief. He'll say "see, action is what matters, we don't need political ed." The essay won't help me answer him because the essay gives him no texture either.

## The organizer is passive-voiced out of the picture

**severity:** material
**summary:** "Someone drags you to a meeting" — who? The essay's thesis depends on an organizer who never appears in the sentence.

The piece's model is action → belonging → belief → reason. Fine. But every verb is in the wrong voice. "Someone drags you." "A person there knew your name." "The canvasser knocks twenty doors." There is no one in this essay who *builds* the canvass. No one who *makes the list* of twenty doors — which targeting decision was political education before it was action. No one who *ran the debrief* where the canvasser learned what to say. No one who *trained her* not to argue.

This is the quiet sophistry I did not expect from a piece this careful. The writer diagnoses the expert-class for missing that belief is load-bearing, then writes a theory of action that does not include the labor of constructing the rooms where action happens. In doing so he accidentally sides with the half of my co-organizers I'm trying to argue against: the ones who say "just run campaigns, the work teaches itself." I know from eight years that it does not teach itself. Somebody teaches it.

What I wanted from this essay was ammunition that says: *yes, action over argument AND this is why we need to train organizers to build action correctly.* What I got was: action over argument AND the organizer is invisible. That collapse is material for me.

## "He was right"

**severity:** minor
**summary:** too charitable to the expert's factual position — being right about the topic isn't the same as being right about the situation

I've watched the expert be wrong about the factual thing too, more often than this essay grants. The tenant who "parroted a mistruth" may have been working off a heuristic that's 70% right — landlords cheat, the city sides with developers, the rule is rigged. The expert's "correction" may have been technically accurate about the particular rule while being dead wrong about the shape of the world the spreader was naming. The piece gives the expert the factual high ground to make its point about rhetoric. It could have made the same point while granting that these arguments are usually ALSO factually muddier than they look.

## "Every modern culture war reruns this"

**severity:** minor
**summary:** over-generalizes Bismarck's loss — sometimes pushing works, and the essay owes us the distinction

Bismarck's mistake wasn't pressing on values. It was pressing on values without moving material conditions. A rent strike presses on values (landlord-tenant relation, who the neighborhood belongs to) AND moves material conditions at the same time, and rent strikes win. The "every modern culture war reruns this" claim smuggles in that pressing always backfires. It doesn't. Pressing backfires when the pressure is purely symbolic. The essay has the pieces to make this sharper — it chose the bigger, flatter claim instead.

## Solidarity vs critical reasoning as "opposed forces"

**severity:** minor
**summary:** insight is right; framing flattens a real cadence organizers actually practice

The observation is true in the abstract. In an organizing body it's more like rhythm than opposition. There's a time for critical reasoning (strategy, debriefs, one-on-ones with leadership) and a time when it kills the room (the moment before an action, in front of members, after a decision has been made collectively). Good organizers hold both. Bad ones collapse one into the other. The essay flattens this into "opposed forces." A footnote or one more paragraph would recover the rhythm.

## The writer's own location

**severity:** minor
**summary:** I can't tell where the "I" is standing — in the kitchen or reading about the kitchen

You get credit for "a rent that drops, a job that returns, a neighbor who shows up with groceries when the power is out." That line has material in it. You lose a little credit for the theorist register ("centrifugal," the Kulturkampf deep cut) sitting next to a single BBQ anecdote as the piece's empirical base. Where has the writer been? If he's been in a union hall or a tenant meeting, a single line of that would earn the analytic voice its weight. If he hasn't, the analytic voice needs to wear its limits more openly. Right now the register and the evidence base don't quite match.

## "Normcore's core norms" → "Normative Statements"

**severity:** nit
**summary:** too cute for what follows

The piece earns its seriousness by "Hearts and Minds." The opening header is a wink it doesn't need. I forgot it by the Kulturkampf section, which means it did no work.

## "I hadn't heard the term 'culture war' until May 2025"

**severity:** nit
**summary:** bold claim, raised an eyebrow, didn't stop

Fine. Raised an eyebrow on the way through. The communities the writer frequents are evidently not the ones I organize in.

## The door-knock line

**severity:** nit
**summary:** keep it — this is the essay's one moment of the real

"She changes one or two by being the first person that month who asked the resident what was hard about his week and meant it." This is the sentence I'll remember. It's the texture the rest of the essay is missing. If the writer can write this sentence, he can write five more like it and give me the essay I wanted.
