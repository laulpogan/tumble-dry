## Redrafted artifact

# The Tyranny Of The Real

I was recently at a barbecue with friends when someone parroted a mistruth they'd heard on the media. Another individual there is something of an expert on this topic in local politics and immediately sank their teeth into the misinformation spreader and didn't let go. Things grew increasingly uncomfortable as the person who had forwarded the misinformation backpedalled and admitted they weren't really familiar with the situation, they were just repeating something a friend had told them. The more informed individual berated them for passing on information they weren't sure about, and contributing to the problem.

We live in a world in which facts have political polarity, and the job of sorting them has been quietly reassigned to the individual, who mostly declines it.

When I later asked the informed person about the blow up, they said "it's people like that who are to blame for politics going to shit. They should check their facts before they repeat them."

Let's talk about "should".

"Should" is what you reach for when you want the world to be different than it is. It is the vocabulary of the scold. It is also, empirically, the vocabulary of the loser — nobody was ever argued into changing their mind by being told what they *should* have done. The expert at the barbecue won the argument and lost the room. He was right. He also made certain the spreader would tell the story later as *that guy at the barbecue*, not *the guy who set me straight*.

## *Kulturkampf*

I hadn't paid much attention to the term "culture war" until May 2025 (about a year before I'm writing this), when a brief spike in its popularity pushed it into several communities I frequent. I'd heard it; I'd filed it under slogan. What I hadn't done was sit with the loan word under it.

A culture war is a struggle for the moral soul of a population. Culture war is not about material reality — it is about *values*.

The original *Kulturkampf* was Otto von Bismarck's attempt, in the 1870s, to subordinate the Roman Catholic Church to the newly unified German state. It was not a single decree but a rolling set of instruments: the Jesuit Law of 1872 expelling the order; the *Maigesetze* of 1873 placing clerical training, appointments, and ecclesiastical discipline under Prussian oversight; civil marriage introduced in Prussia in 1874 and extended across the Reich the year after.

Bismarck is the patron saint of *Realpolitik* — the term itself was Ludwig von Rochau's, coined in 1853, but Bismarck is who most people mean when they say it. The *Kulturkampf* is where that instinct broke against something it could not move. The laws were not dramatically repealed; they were quietly wound down, unenforced, traded away through the 1880s, by which point Bismarck was negotiating votes with the Centre Party — the *Zentrum*, founded in December 1870, before the campaign began, and which his campaign turned into a disciplined mass organization. The Catholic population he attempted to discipline did not become more German, more modern, more obedient. It became more Catholic, better organized, and politically durable for another half-century.

The lesson is not that Bismarck "lost." He was too careful a player to lose cleanly. The lesson is that pushing on a population's values through state pressure produced the opposite of what it intended. The harder the *Kulturkampf* pressed, the more cohesive the thing it pressed on became.

Every modern culture war reruns this, and the rerun does not care which team holds the pressure. Whoever holds institutional power in a given fight — a state, a platform, a university, an HR department, an editorial board, a school board, a state legislature writing curriculum law, a sheriff's office deciding which protest to tolerate — finds that *telling* a population what to believe produces a population that believes harder in the opposite thing, now with grievance on its side. The mechanism is indifferent to the jersey of the institution wielding it. The technocrat does not convert the populist. He constitutes him. The culture warrior on the other side, legislating against drag shows or banning books, does not convert the liberal teenager. She constitutes her.

The distinction Bismarck missed, and that modern versions keep missing, is this: pressing on values *while also* moving material conditions is a different operation than pressing on values alone. A rent strike presses on values — who the neighborhood belongs to, what a landlord is owed — and it moves the rent. Rent strikes win sometimes. The *Kulturkampf* pressed on values and moved nothing a Catholic villager could feel in her week. That is why it produced cohesion instead of conversion.

## Hearts and Minds

The counterinsurgency phrase "hearts and minds" is usually delivered as if the two words were one thing. They are not. "Hearts" is what a losing project tries to win when it hopes sentiment can paper over material failure. "Minds" moves when conditions move. The COIN literature itself is not as clean on this as the phrase implies — in practice "hearts and minds" campaigns that worked paired clinics and wells with the leaflets, and the ones that stayed at the leaflet layer failed. So take this split as a claim about the cases where hearts gets asked to do all the work alone.

The expert at the barbecue was fighting for a heart. He believed, sincerely, that if he just pushed hard enough, the spreader would see. He was mistaken about the geometry of the problem. The spreader's belief was not sitting on top of his life like a hat he could swap for a better one. It was load-bearing. It did a job: it let him feel oriented, it let him belong, it gave him a story that explained why his rent went up and his job got worse.

You cannot argue someone out of a load-bearing belief. You can only change what it is loading.

## Solidarity and critical reasoning are opposed forces

In the abstract, critical reasoning is a virtue. Inside a group, it is centrifugal. The person at the meeting who keeps raising objections, who insists on evaluating each claim on its merits, who is always asking *but is that true* — they are also the person no one trusts with the hard work. Solidarity requires a suspension of the critical faculty directed at your own coalition. Not forever, not on everything, but enough of the time that the group can move as a group.

This is a rhythm, not a rule. Organizers I've watched run the critical-reasoning channel hard in the places it belongs — strategy meetings, debriefs, one-on-ones with leadership before a decision is made. They close it down in the places it kills the room — the moment before an action, in front of new members, after a decision has been taken and the job is to execute. The person who doesn't know which moment is which is the person no one trusts with the hard work.

This is why expert-class interventions into working-class politics fail on contact. The expert arrives with a checklist of corrections and is surprised when the room closes ranks. The room is not defending the misinformation. It is defending the right to not be audited by an outsider in its own kitchen.

## Reciprocating Cycles

The liberal story about belief goes: you read something, you think about it, you update your views, you act differently. Reason → belief → action.

The real order is closer to: action → belonging → belief → reason. A tenant organizer in your building knocks, says she heard the landlord is pushing a 9% increase and they're meeting in the lobby Thursday, and would you come. You go because she knocked, not because you've decided anything. At the meeting a neighbor you've nodded at for two years tells a story about her boiler. You come back the next week because she asked if you would. After a few months you find yourself explaining the building's position to a skeptical cousin, and you notice, surprised, that you mean it. The reasons arrive last, not first, dressed as the conclusions of an argument you believe you conducted yourself.

This is how unions recruit: a steward who's worked the same line for eight years walks over the first morning after someone gets written up and says *here's what they did to me in '19, here's what we did back.* The write-up is the action; the walk-over is the recruitment. This is how churches recruit: a deacon shows up with a casserole three days after the funeral, when the other casseroles have stopped, and sits at the table for an hour without being asked anything. The grief is the condition; the casserole is the recruitment. This is how a canvass recruits: the organizer who trained the canvasser spent six months building the turf — mapping the blocks, recruiting the volunteers, writing the rap, running the role-plays in the church basement on Tuesday nights — before any door got knocked. The canvasser who knocks twenty doors in a night doesn't change twenty minds by arguing. She changes one or two by being the first person that month who asked the resident what was hard about his week and meant it. She can do that because someone else built the night she's walking into.

Minds won through action act. Minds won through reason reason. The difference shows up on the third Tuesday, when it's raining and the meeting is still at seven.

## What this means if you're not already a steward

I've been dodging the word, so let me stop. The field I'm describing is *organizing* — the craft of getting a room of people who share a condition to act together on it. Unions do it. Tenant associations do it. Churches do it. Mutual aid networks do it. Political campaigns do it when they are being honest, and do something else when they are not. If you're reading this and nodding, the thing you're nodding at has a name and several hundred years of practice behind it, and the name is organizing.

Which means "action" is not a mood. It has a shape, and the shape is small. For the reader who isn't a steward or a deacon or a trained canvasser — the reader at the barbecue, wishing they had said something other than what the expert said — "action" looks like this:

- You pick one condition you share with the people near you. The rent. The shift schedule. The stretch of road the school bus can't turn around on. Something material, something local, something a neighbor would recognize as their problem too.
- You find one other person who has it, and you ask them about it. Not to convince them of anything. To hear how they describe it in their own words. The point of the first conversation is the second conversation.
- You show up before you are asked to. The casserole, the ride to the meeting, the text the morning after the write-up. The walk-over is the recruitment. The knock is the knock.
- You do it again next week. The difference shows up on the third Tuesday.

That's the Monday version. It is not a program and it is not a theory of change. It is the smallest unit of the thing the essay has been describing the whole time, handed to a reader who wanted to know what to do with their hands.

---

Most humans don't change their views in response to well-reasoned arguments. They latch on to the first view that seeds their belief space and defend it. What moves people, more reliably than argument, is *material reality* — a rent that drops, a job that returns, a neighbor who shows up with groceries when the power is out. This is not a mystical claim. The political-science shorthand for it is economic retrospective voting: voters punish and reward incumbents based on how their lives have gone on the incumbent's watch. It is one of the most robust findings in the field, though honest readers of the literature (Achen and Bartels on the skeptical end; the retrospective-voting revival on the other) will tell you it's contested at the edges, that attribution matters, that voters credit and blame imperfectly. The claim I need for this essay is the weaker one that survives all of that: conditions move minds more reliably than messaging does.

The tyranny of the real is that it does not care what you should believe. It cares what you have done, what has been done to you, and what is being done in your name. The expert at the barbecue won his argument. He did not change the mind. He never will, by that method. The mind will change when the thing the belief was loading gets smaller, or when something else can carry the weight — and the something else, in every case I've watched it happen, was a person who showed up before they were asked to.
