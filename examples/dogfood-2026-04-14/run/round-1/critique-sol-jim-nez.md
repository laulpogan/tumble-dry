# Critique — Sol Jiménez

**Persona:** Tenant organizer, 8 years in the work, reads theory at night, looking for ammunition in a long-running fight with her co-organizers about action vs. political education.
**Overall:** The thesis is my side of the argument — "minds won through action act" is literally the sentence I'd paste into the group chat. And the piece gives me nothing to back it up with. No fight, no campaign, no door, no eviction, no win, no loss. Just a barbeque. I can't bring this to a meeting. It would get laughed out of the room.

## Assumptions stress-test

- **Assumption 1 (the expert-berating-the-spreader is the interesting problem):** Partial grant. I've watched this exact dynamic chew through new canvassers for eight years — the earnest one who knows the numbers annihilating some tenant who parroted a rumor about the landlord. It's real. But the piece treats it as a site of curiosity, not a problem to be solved, and it happens at a *barbeque*, not on a doorstep or in a union hall. So I grant the dynamic exists and deny that this anecdote is the place to examine it.
- **Assumption 2 (material reality, not reasoning, changes minds):** Full grant. This is my belief. It's why I'm still doing this work. I will defend this claim against my own co-organizers any day of the week. That's exactly why I'm so disappointed — the writer asserts it in the tone of a settled matter and does nothing to earn it. I don't need convincing; I need a version I can hand to Miguel who DOESN'T already believe it, and this isn't it.
- **Assumption 3 (the Kulturkampf analogy is illuminating):** Deny. I don't care who Bismarck was and neither does anyone I organize with. If I have to know 19th-century Prussian church politics to follow your argument about modern culture war, you've picked the wrong example. There are a thousand closer fights — any rent strike, any union drive, any ballot initiative — that would map cleaner and mean more. The loanword is doing decoration work.

## Findings

## No skin in the game — the author has never lost a campaign
**severity:** material
**summary:** Piece makes an organizing-adjacent claim but shows no evidence of organizing experience; its only "field" is a dinner party.

The terminal claim of this essay is that material reality changes minds. Fine. Where is yours? Where is the author's? The entire empirical base of this piece is one barbeque anecdote between friends who apparently already move in the same social world. Not a door-knock. Not a strike vote. Not a single interaction with someone whose housing or wages were on the line. I read the whole piece waiting for the author to cash the check — to tell me about a canvass where someone flipped, or a fight they lost because they argued instead of organized — and it never came. For a piece whose thesis is "action beats argument," it is embarrassingly all argument and no action. If the writer had shown me one real scene from one real fight I would have forgiven every empty header. They didn't. It reads as erudition in cosplay.

## "Action" never gets named
**severity:** material
**summary:** The load-bearing word "action" is left abstract; the piece never specifies what kind of action does the work minds are supposedly won through.

"Minds won through action act" — great, I'll take it. What action? A rent strike? A mutual aid network? A ballot canvass? A one-on-one at someone's kitchen table where you shut up for forty minutes and let them talk? A picket? The answers are not the same and they do not produce the same kind of person on the other side. This is the whole argument I'm having with my co-organizers. Some of them think a rally is action. Some think a mailer is action. Some think a study group is action. The essay's closing aphorism does not distinguish between any of these, which means it can't settle the fight — it just gets quoted by whichever side likes the cadence of the sentence. A piece that actually believed in action would name some.

## Nothing an organizer can use
**severity:** material
**summary:** Piece agrees with my thesis but provides no examples, cases, or vocabulary I can carry into a disagreement.

I came here looking for ammunition. I got rhetoric. When I fight with Miguel about whether we should run another political-education workshop or just put 20 more people on doors, I need to be able to say: *here's a campaign, here's what happened, here's what actually moved people, and here's the generalization that follows.* This piece lets me gesture at an aphorism. That's worse than nothing — because if I show up with only aphorisms, I confirm Miguel's read that my side of the argument is vibes.

## The framing of "fact-checking is an individual responsibility" is the wrong target
**severity:** minor
**summary:** Author accepts the liberal-epistemics frame in order to critique it; I'd rather they reject it outright.

"Fact-checking is an increasingly individual responsibility few take on" — this sentence is doing the opposite of what the author wants. It sounds regretful, as if the problem is that people aren't being conscientious enough individual fact-checkers. The whole point of the piece (I think?) is that individual-epistemic-virtue is not how political change works. So why land on a sentence that mourns its decline? This is the kind of framing my comfortable-liberal donors use. I spend a lot of time trying to get people OUT of that frame. I don't need writers on my side smuggling it back in.

## The barbeque is the author's social world, and it shows
**severity:** minor
**summary:** The choice of setting tells the reader who the author organizes with, which is no one.

Organizers don't meet people at barbeques with friends. Organizers meet people they disagree with on purpose. The fact that the author's representative political interaction is a dinner party between people already in the same circles is itself a tell. I clocked it by paragraph two. It doesn't kill the piece, but it sets the ceiling for how seriously I can take the rest.

## Kulturkampf section is name-dropping and I skimmed it
**severity:** minor
**summary:** Bismarck / Centre Party detour does no analogical work; a closer example from contemporary practice would do the same job in a third the words.

I read theory, so I'm not bouncing off "Kulturkampf" the word — I'm bouncing off the fact that it's doing nothing. The author tells me Bismarck lost and that it united his enemies as a voting bloc. Fine. Who's Bismarck now? Which coalition unites? What "losing" looks like in 2025? The mapping isn't drawn so the history is decoration. A paragraph about the Montgomery bus boycott would do the same structural work and wouldn't require me to remember which German chancellor did what in 1890.

## The expert-berating-the-spreader scene was almost a real thing
**severity:** minor
**summary:** The strongest moment in the piece is flattened by the author's refusal to take it somewhere.

This is the part I almost respected. Experts annihilating the uninformed is a thing I deal with constantly in organizing — the new volunteer who knows the policy analysis and cannot stop themselves from humiliating the tenant who doesn't. It destroys recruitment. It makes our side unbearable. The author noticed the dynamic. Then they walked away from it, into Bismarck. If they'd stayed in that room — what did the berated person do the next day, did they still vote, did they still come back to community events — they would have had an essay. Instead, the scene becomes a springboard into abstraction, which is exactly the move the essay's own thesis is supposed to be against.

## Empty section headers
**severity:** nit
**summary:** Stub headings read as unfinished rather than deliberate, but I'd forgive them if the rest were earning its keep.

"Hearts and Minds" and "Solidarity and critical reasoning are opposed forces" with nothing under them. I was told to forgive style. I'll forgive it. But I'll note that "Solidarity and critical reasoning are opposed forces" is a hell of a claim to make as a section heading and then not defend — especially in a piece about whether reason or action wins minds. That's the argument. Put it in the body.

## "Normcore's core norms"
**severity:** nit
**summary:** Cute header, no connection to essay content, not worth its slot.

I don't know what normcore has to do with culture war and the piece isn't going to tell me. Cut it or earn it.
