# Assumption Audit — Round 1

The piece takes the following for granted. Reviewers: stress-test any you think
the target audience will not grant.

## About the reader

1. Reader already understands "normcore" — **evidence:** it appears as a section heading ("Normcore's core norms") with no definition and no connection drawn to the essay's actual content.
2. Reader is fluent enough with German loanwords and 19th-century European history to find "Kulturkampf" and Bismarck evocative rather than alienating — **evidence:** the piece offers a two-sentence summary of the original Kulturkampf and then relies on the reader to feel its "poignance" without ever spelling out the analogy.
3. Reader knows what "Realpolitik" means and recognizes Bismarck as its founding figure — **evidence:** "the OG of *Realpolitik,* Otto von Bismarck" is dropped as flavor, not explained.
4. Reader shares the author's implicit sympathy with the misinformation spreader and discomfort with the expert — **evidence:** the expert is described as having "sank their teeth in" and "didn't let go" and "berated" the other party; the spreader merely "backpedalled." The affective loading points one direction and is never justified.
5. Reader tolerates (even expects) an essayistic form with empty sections, section headings that function as gestures, and an ending on an aphorism rather than a conclusion — **evidence:** "Hearts and Minds" and "Solidarity and critical reasoning are opposed forces" are headings with no body; the piece ends "Minds won through action act. Minds won through reason reason." with no synthesis.
6. Reader is already inside the author's intellectual milieu — **evidence:** "several communities I frequent" is cited as the unit of cultural observation without identifying which communities or why the reader should treat that sample as signal.
7. Reader has the patience to read a 600-word meandering essay whose thesis is never stated explicitly — **evidence:** the piece opens with an anecdote, pivots to "Let's talk about 'should'" and then never returns to "should."

## About the subject

1. The BBQ anecdote is representative of something larger, not a one-off — **evidence:** a single dinner-party exchange is generalized into "We live in a world in which facts have political polarity" in the very next sentence.
2. "Should" is a philosophically loaded move worth pausing on — **evidence:** "Let's talk about 'should'" is treated as a pivot, but the promised discussion never arrives; the reader is asked to grant that it would have been interesting.
3. Solidarity and critical reasoning are in genuine tension — **evidence:** the section heading "Solidarity and critical reasoning are opposed forces" asserts the claim as a fact and then provides zero argument, text, or example.
4. The Kulturkampf/culture-war analogy is actually illuminating — **evidence:** the piece calls the loan "poignant" and notes Bismarck lost, but never maps which modern faction is Bismarck, which is the Catholic Church, or why losing the Kulturkampf should inform how we read today's conflicts.
5. Most humans don't change their minds via well-reasoned argument; they change via material reality — **evidence:** stated flatly under "Reciprocating Cycles" as if settled ("Most humans don't change their views... What gets people to change their views is *material reality*"), with no citation, example, or argument.
6. "Minds won through action act. Minds won through reason reason." is self-evidently meaningful — **evidence:** the aphorism closes the piece and is asked to carry the weight of a conclusion.
7. The fact-checking-as-individual-responsibility claim is a problem — **evidence:** "fact-checking is an increasingly individual responsibility few take on" is delivered in a tone of regret, assuming the reader agrees this framing is the trouble.

## About the context

1. Reader knows what year "may of this year" refers to — **evidence:** the piece says the term "culture war" spiked "in may of this year" with no anchor date; the essay is undated internally.
2. Reader is broadly aware of recent "culture war" discourse and its vocabulary — **evidence:** "culture war" is introduced as if only mildly new to the author, but the reader is expected to have enough prior exposure to recognize the stakes being invoked.
3. The author-reader relationship already has enough trust that first-person anecdote counts as evidence — **evidence:** the essay opens with an unsourced personal story and uses it to found a claim about the state of the world.
4. Empty/stub section headings will be read as deliberate rather than as an unfinished draft — **evidence:** "Hearts and Minds" has no body; the piece is presented as finished prose.
5. Reader is reading for mood and gesture, not for a testable argument — **evidence:** the piece's structural choices (stub sections, pivot that doesn't resolve, closing aphorism) only work if the reader treats it as a vibe-piece rather than an essay with a claim.

## Load-bearing rank

The top 3 assumptions most likely to make or break this piece with its audience:

1. The reader grants the framing that the expert-berating-the-misinformation-spreader is the problem worth examining (not the misinformation itself). If the reader instinctively sides with the expert, the entire essay lands as sophistry defending bad epistemic behavior.
2. The reader accepts on assertion that material reality — not reasoned argument — is what changes minds. This is the essay's terminal claim, presented without argument; if the reader doesn't already believe it, the piece has given them no reason to start.
3. The reader accepts the Kulturkampf analogy as illuminating without the mapping being drawn. If the reader demands to know who in the modern culture war is Bismarck and what "losing" would look like, the historical section collapses into name-dropping.
