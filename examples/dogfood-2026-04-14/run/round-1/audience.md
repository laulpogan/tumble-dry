# Audience Panel — Round 1

**Inferred purpose:** An essay arguing that moralistic "fact-checking" and reasoned argument are the wrong tools for culture-war disagreements because minds are changed by material reality and action, not by being told what they "should" believe.

**Inferred primary audience:** Politically-engaged intellectual-blogosphere readers — the Substack / Slow Boring / post-rationalist axis — who enjoy essays that braid a personal anecdote, a historical loanword, and a contrarian thesis about discourse.

## Panel

### 1. Mariana Kobayashi — Senior Policy Staffer, State Legislature (8 yrs)
**Hiring job:** Reading on a Sunday morning to find a frame for why her door-knocking canvassers keep bouncing off voters who "should" know better. Subscribes to Slow Boring, Yglesias, and a rotating Substack stack; she's the nominal target reader.
**Bounces if:** The piece stalls out before delivering its payload. The half-filled section headings ("Hearts and Minds", "Solidarity and critical reasoning are opposed forces", "Reciprocating Cycles") read as a draft, not a post — if the skeleton isn't filled in by the time she hits them, she closes the tab and feels slightly insulted that it was published at all.
**Brings to the table:** A field-earned conviction that the Most Online discourse about fact-checking is irrelevant to the people she actually needs to move. She'll nod at the BBQ anecdote and then wait — impatiently — for the punchline the writer has promised but not yet delivered.

### 2. Dr. Paul Henning — Historian of 19th-century Central Europe, small-press author
**Hiring job:** Clicked through from a Twitter share because "Kulturkampf" is in the title and he has a Google alert on the term. He wants to see whether the writer earned the metaphor or just borrowed its suit.
**Bounces if:** The historical claim doesn't land. "Bismarck lost the Kulturkampf" is a defensible reading but it needs nuance — Zentrum consolidated as a bloc, yes, but most of the May Laws were quietly wound down rather than repealed under pressure, and the "loss" was partial and strategic. If the piece treats Kulturkampf as vibe-rhyme with the present instead of a real argument, he'll write a politely devastating two-paragraph response on his own blog.
**Brings to the table:** A strong prior that historical analogy is usually abused to pretty up contemporary takes. He's watched "Kulturkampf" get weaponized by both the trad-right and the post-liberal left this year and he's tired of it.

### 3. Sol Jiménez — Tenant organizer (8 yrs), reads theory at night
**Hiring job:** Looking for intellectual backup for a long-running fight with her co-organizers about whether political education is a waste of time versus just running more campaigns. The thesis "minds won through action act" sounds like her side of the argument and she wants ammunition.
**Bounces if:** "Action" stays an abstraction. She wants the writer to name what kind of action does the work — strikes? mutual aid? electoral canvassing? unglamorous one-on-ones? If the essay stays in essay-mode and never touches organizing reality, she loses interest around "Reciprocating Cycles" and files the piece under "theorists writing about practice."
**Brings to the table:** A deep-in-the-bones belief that most political writing is done by people who have never lost a campaign. She will forgive a lot — including the half-finished headers — if the writer shows they've had skin in a real fight. If not, the erudition reads as cosplay.
