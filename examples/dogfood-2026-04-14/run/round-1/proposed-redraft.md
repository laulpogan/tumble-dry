# The Tyranny Of The Real

## Normcore's core norms

Normative Statements

I was recently at a barbeque with friends when someone parroted a mistruth they'd heard on the media. Another individual there is something of an expert on this topic in local politics and immediately sank their teeth into the misinformation spreader and didn't let go. Things grew increasingly uncomfortable as the person who had forwarded the misinformation backpedalled and admitted they weren't really familiar with the situation, they were just repeating something a friend had told them. The more informed individual berated them for passing on information they weren't sure about, and contributing to the problem.

We live in a world in which facts have political polarity, and the job of sorting them has been quietly reassigned to the individual, who mostly declines it.

When I later asked the informed person about the blow up, they said "it's people like that who are to blame for politics going to shit. They should check their facts before they repeat them."

Let's talk about "should".

"Should" is what you reach for when you want the world to be different than it is. It is the vocabulary of the scold. It is also, empirically, the vocabulary of the loser — nobody was ever argued into changing their mind by being told what they *should* have done. The expert at the barbeque won the argument and lost the room. He was right. He also made certain the spreader would tell the story later as *that guy at the barbeque*, not *the guy who set me straight*.

## *Kulturkampf*

I hadn't heard the term "culture war" until May 2025, when a brief spike in popularity brought it to the attention of several communities I frequent.

A culture war is a struggle for the moral soul of a population. Culture war is not about material reality — it is about *values*.

The origin of the term as a loan from the German *Kulturkampf* is worth sitting with. The original *Kulturkampf* was Otto von Bismarck's attempt, in the 1870s, to subordinate the Roman Catholic Church to the newly unified German state. He expelled the Jesuits, put civil marriage under state control, and through the *Maigesetze* — the May Laws of 1873 — placed clerical training and appointments under Prussian supervision.

Bismarck is the OG of *Realpolitik,* patron saint of doing whatever works. The *Kulturkampf* is where that instinct broke against something it could not move. The May Laws were not dramatically repealed; they were quietly wound down, unenforced, traded away through the 1880s, by which point Bismarck was negotiating votes with the same Centre Party his campaign had brought into existence. The Catholic population he attempted to discipline did not become more German, more modern, more obedient. It became more Catholic, better organized, and politically durable for another half-century.

The lesson is not that Bismarck "lost." He was too careful a player to lose cleanly. The lesson is that pushing on a population's values through state pressure produced the opposite of what it intended. The harder the *Kulturkampf* pressed, the more cohesive the thing it pressed on became.

Every modern culture war reruns this. Whoever holds institutional power — the state, the platforms, the universities, the HR department, the editorial board — finds that *telling* a population what to believe produces a population that believes harder in the opposite thing, now with grievance on its side. The technocrat does not convert the populist. He constitutes him.

## Hearts and Minds

The counterinsurgency phrase "hearts and minds" is usually delivered as if the two words were one thing. They are not. "Hearts" is what a losing project tries to win when it hopes sentiment can paper over material failure. "Minds" moves when conditions move.

The expert at the barbeque was fighting for a heart. He believed, sincerely, that if he just pushed hard enough, the spreader would see. He was mistaken about the geometry of the problem. The spreader's belief was not sitting on top of his life like a hat he could swap for a better one. It was load-bearing. It did a job: it let him feel oriented, it let him belong, it gave him a story that explained why his rent went up and his job got worse.

You cannot argue someone out of a load-bearing belief. You can only change what it is loading.

## Solidarity and critical reasoning are opposed forces

In the abstract, critical reasoning is a virtue. Inside a group, it is centrifugal. The person at the meeting who keeps raising objections, who insists on evaluating each claim on its merits, who is always asking *but is that true* — they are also the person no one trusts with the hard work. Solidarity requires a suspension of the critical faculty directed at your own coalition. Not forever, not on everything, but enough of the time that the group can move as a group.

This is why expert-class interventions into working-class politics fail on contact. The expert arrives with a checklist of corrections and is surprised when the room closes ranks. The room is not defending the misinformation. It is defending the right to not be audited by an outsider in its own kitchen.

## Reciprocating Cycles

The liberal story about belief goes: you read something, you think about it, you update your views, you act differently. Reason → belief → action.

The real order is closer to: action → belonging → belief → reason. Someone drags you to a meeting. You come back because a person there knew your name. After a few months you find yourself explaining the group's priorities to a skeptical cousin, and you notice, surprised, that you mean it. The reasons arrive last, not first, dressed as the conclusions of an argument you believe you conducted yourself.

This is how unions recruit. It is how churches recruit. It is how a canvass recruits. It is how anything that actually moves people recruits. The canvasser who knocks twenty doors in a night doesn't change twenty minds by arguing. She changes one or two by being the first person that month who asked the resident what was hard about his week and meant it.

Minds won through action act. Minds won through reason reason.

---

Most humans don't change their views in response to well-reasoned arguments. They latch on to the first view that seeds their belief space and defend it. What gets people to change their views is *material reality* — a rent that drops, a job that returns, a neighbor who shows up with groceries when the power is out. This is not a mystical claim. The political-science shorthand for it is economic retrospective voting, and it is the single most reliable predictor anyone has found of which coalition an ordinary voter breaks for, stronger than any combination of messaging variables yet measured.

The tyranny of the real is that it does not care what you should believe. It cares what you have done, what has been done to you, and what is being done in your name. The expert at the barbeque won his argument. He did not change the mind. He never will, by that method. The mind will change when the thing the belief was loading gets smaller, or when something else can carry the weight.
